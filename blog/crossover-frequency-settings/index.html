Crossover Frequency Settings — How to Set for DJ

📅 24 April 2026
Englishहिंदी

A crossover divides the audio signal into frequency bands — sending bass to subwoofers, mids to mid-range speakers, and highs to tweeters. Getting the crossover frequency settings wrong is one of the most common reasons DJ sound systems sound harsh, boomy, or distorted in India. This guide explains exactly how to set crossover frequencies for professional DJ results.

Why Crossover Frequency Matters

Every speaker driver has a range it's designed to reproduce. Forcing a tweeter to play bass frequencies will destroy it. Forcing a subwoofer to play high frequencies wastes power and sounds terrible. The crossover protects your drivers and ensures each handles only the frequencies it was designed for — resulting in clearer, louder, and more efficient sound.

Types of Crossover Points

  • Low-Pass Filter (LPF): Passes only frequencies BELOW the set point to the subwoofer. Set this to 80–100Hz for most DJ setups.
  • High-Pass Filter (HPF): Passes only frequencies ABOVE the set point to the main speaker. Set this to 80–100Hz (matching the LPF) to eliminate the frequency overlap.
  • Bandpass: Used for mid-range drivers — passes a band between a high-pass and low-pass point.

Recommended Crossover Settings for Indian DJ Setups

2-Way Setup (Subwoofer + Full-Range Main)

This is the most common Indian DJ configuration. Set your crossover network plate LPF at 80Hz for the subwoofer and HPF at 80Hz for the main speaker. This is clean, safe, and works for virtually all Bollywood and Punjabi music.

3-Way Setup (Sub + Mid + High)

Sub LPF: 80Hz | Mid HPF: 80Hz, Mid LPF: 2500Hz | High HPF: 2500Hz. This configuration (supported by Punishka Trading's crossover network plates) gives the cleanest sound for large events and concerts.

Crossover Slope — 12dB vs 24dB

The slope determines how sharply the filter cuts off frequencies above/below the crossover point. A 24dB/octave slope is steeper and more protective for your drivers — recommended for high-powered DJ setups. A 12dB slope is gentler and more musical, preferred for hi-fi applications. Most DJ crossover plates at Punishka Trading offer 12dB or 24dB switchable slopes.

Step-by-Step: Setting Your Crossover

  1. Connect your mixer output to the crossover input
  2. Connect the crossover's LPF output to your subwoofer amplifier
  3. Connect the HPF output to your main speaker amplifier (PU8000 or similar)
  4. Set LPF and HPF both to 80Hz to start
  5. Play music and listen — if bass is too heavy, lower LPF to 60Hz. If thin, raise to 100Hz
  6. Adjust gain on each amplifier channel independently for balanced output

Common Mistakes

  • Mismatched crossover points — sub LPF at 120Hz, main HPF at 80Hz creates a "hole" in the sound
  • Setting crossover too high — sending 200Hz+ to the subwoofer makes bass sound boomy and directional
  • Ignoring phase — if sub and mains are out of phase, bass cancels. Use the phase switch on the crossover
