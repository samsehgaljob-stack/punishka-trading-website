What is an XLR Cable?

XLR is a 3-pin balanced audio connector — pin 1 is ground, pin 2 is hot (+), pin 3 is cold (−). Because it uses balanced signaling, external noise cancels out, which is why every professional microphone, DSP and powered speaker input uses XLR. The "male" end goes into the mic, the "female" end into the mixer or DSP.

Why Balanced Beats Unbalanced

A standard 1/4" TS or RCA cable picks up noise from nearby power lines, LED dimmers and WiFi routers — very common at Indian wedding venues. A balanced XLR rejects this noise almost completely, giving you clean vocal and instrument signal even over 20-meter runs.

Wire Gauge and Build Quality

  • 24 AWG conductor: Entry-level, fine for short indoor runs under 5 m.
  • 22 AWG conductor: Standard for DJ and stage use, runs up to 15 m with no loss.
  • 20 AWG oxygen-free copper (OFC): Pro grade, handles 30 m+ runs, lower resistance, better shielding.

Always check for a dense braided shield (not just foil), flexible PVC or rubber jacket, and properly soldered (not crimped) Neutrik-style connectors.

Length — How Much Do You Need?

  • 1 m: Mixer-to-DSP patching
  • 3 m: Wireless receiver to mixer
  • 5 m: Stage mic to snake
  • 10 m: Standard DJ mic cable
  • 15–20 m: Long wedding stage runs
  • 30 m+: Outdoor events, requires pro OFC build

Connectors — What to Look For

Cheap XLR connectors use plastic strain relief that cracks within 3 months of tent-house use. Look for metal housing, gold-plated pins (resists corrosion in monsoon humidity), and a screw-lock cable clamp. Neutrik-style connectors are the industry benchmark — 10x the lifespan of no-brand units.

Common Problems & Fixes

  • Hum when you move the cable: Broken shield — replace cable.
  • Intermittent dropouts: Loose solder joint — re-solder or replace connector.
  • One-sided loud hiss: Pin 2 or 3 short — check continuity.
  • Ground loop hum (steady 50 Hz): Ground issue in building wiring — use a ground-lift box, never cut the ground pin.
